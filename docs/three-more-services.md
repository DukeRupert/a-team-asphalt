### Intro

Your driveway takes more abuse than most people realize. Vehicle weight, freeze-thaw cycling, rainwater drainage, surface oxidation — it handles all of it, year after year. Done right, an asphalt driveway lasts twenty to twenty-five years with basic maintenance. Done wrong, you're calling someone back in five.

The difference almost always comes down to what happens before the asphalt is laid. Subgrade preparation, base depth, compaction, and drainage grade are where driveways succeed or fail. We don't cut those corners. We've seen enough failed driveways to know exactly what causes them — and it's always the prep work.

---

### What's Included

**What this covers:**
- New driveway installation on bare ground or over gravel
- Full driveway replacement (tearout and repave)
- Driveway extension or widening
- Turnarounds and parking aprons
- Proper edge work and border finish
- Grading and drainage to move water away from your home
- Transitions to existing concrete, garage slabs, or street aprons

---

### How We Do It

**Step 01: Site evaluation and grade planning**
Before anything is excavated, we look at how water currently moves on your property. Your driveway needs to drain away from your foundation and not pool at the low end. We plan the grade before we dig.

**Step 02: Excavation and subgrade prep**
We remove existing material to the proper depth for your soil conditions and traffic load. A standard residential driveway needs a minimum of 4 inches of compacted base — more on softer soils. Skimping on depth is the most common reason driveways fail early.

**Step 03: Base installation and compaction**
We bring in the right crushed rock, spread it in lifts, and compact each layer before the next goes down. Compaction matters. Uncompacted base settles unevenly and takes the asphalt surface with it.

**Step 04: Asphalt lay and finish**
Hot mix asphalt goes down at the right temperature and is compacted immediately. We cut clean edges and finish transitions to your garage, street, or existing concrete. No ragged borders, no mismatched heights.

---

### Good to Know

**Good to know:** New asphalt needs to cure before it's sealed — typically 6 to 12 months. The oils in fresh asphalt need to off-gas first. Any contractor who offers to sealcoat your brand-new driveway on the same day is doing you a disservice. We'll tell you when it's ready and come back to seal it properly.

**Good to know:** Puget Sound winters are wet, not brutal — but freeze-thaw cycles still happen, especially in elevated areas of Puyallup, Maple Valley, and Covington. Water that gets under unsupported edges will eventually heave them. We compact the subgrade to minimize this and install edge support where the soil is soft.

**Good to know:** If your old driveway is failing along the edges first, that's almost always a base problem, not a surface problem. An overlay will look fine for a season, then crack along the same lines. We'll tell you honestly whether your driveway is a mill-and-overlay candidate or whether the base needs to come out.

### Intro

Most asphalt doesn't fail from the bottom up — it fails from the surface down. UV oxidation, water infiltration, traffic wear, and Washington's freeze-thaw cycles all work on the top two inches first. If the base beneath it is still structurally sound, tearing the whole thing out is unnecessary.

Milling removes the failed surface layer to a precise, consistent depth. The exposed base is cleaned and inspected, any localized failures are repaired, and fresh hot mix asphalt goes down over a surface that's actually ready to bond to it. The result is effectively new asphalt — for significantly less than full replacement.

It's not the right answer for every situation. If the base has failed, an overlay just delays the inevitable and wastes money. We assess every job honestly before recommending it.

---

### What's Included

**What this covers:**
- Milling (grinding and removal of existing asphalt surface layer)
- Base inspection and spot repair of any sub-base failures
- Tack coat application (bonds new asphalt to the milled surface)
- Hot mix asphalt overlay — typically 2 inches for standard applications
- Edge and transition work
- Haul-off and recycling of milled material
- Striping and finish work (standalone or part of the overlay project)

---

### How We Do It

**Step 01: Assessment — is this lot a milling candidate?**
Before anything else, we evaluate the base. We look at the type and extent of cracking, whether failures are surface or structural, and whether the existing grade and drainage are correct. If the base is compromised in significant areas, we'll tell you — because an overlay over a bad base will mirror the same failures within a season or two.

**Step 02: Milling**
A milling machine grinds the existing asphalt surface down to a consistent depth — typically 2 inches — without disturbing the base beneath it. This removes cracking, surface oxidation, and irregularities, and leaves a textured surface that bonds well to new asphalt. The milled material is loaded and removed for recycling. Asphalt is the most recycled material in the country — nothing goes to a landfill.

**Step 03: Base repair**
Once the old surface is off, we can see exactly what the base looks like. Any soft spots, base failures, or drainage problems that weren't visible from the surface get addressed now — not after the new asphalt is down.

**Step 04: Tack coat**
A tack coat (asphalt emulsion) is applied to the milled surface before paving. It's the bonding agent between old and new. This step is sometimes skipped by contractors looking to save time. Skipping it means the overlay can delaminate under traffic. We don't skip it.

**Step 05: Overlay and compaction**
Fresh hot mix asphalt is laid at the right temperature and immediately compacted. Edges are cut clean and transitions to adjacent surfaces — concrete curbs, garage aprons, street connections — are finished properly.

---

### Good to Know

**Good to know:** The tell-tale sign that milling and overlay is the right call: surface cracking (longitudinal cracks, transverse cracks, edge cracking) without the alligator pattern. Alligator cracking — the web-like, interconnected pattern — indicates base failure. Overlay won't fix it. Surface cracking without that pattern generally means the base is holding and the surface has just worn out.

**Good to know:** An overlay typically adds about 2 inches to the surface height. For most driveways and parking lots this is fine, but it's worth considering at transitions — garage doors, drainage grates, curb cuts, and ADA ramps may need adjustment. We flag these during assessment.

**Good to know:** Milled asphalt is 100% recyclable and is commonly used as aggregate in new asphalt mix. Choosing milling over full replacement is also the more environmentally responsible approach — less material to manufacture, less hauled to disposal.

---

### Milling vs. Full Replacement — When to Choose Each

This is the question we get most often. Here's a plain-language answer:

**Choose milling and overlay when:**
- Surface is cracked, oxidized, or rough but base is solid
- Drainage and grade are correct
- No alligator cracking or widespread base failures
- Budget makes full replacement impractical

**Choose full replacement when:**
- Alligator cracking is widespread (base has failed)
- Pavement is heaving, settling unevenly, or has severe drainage failures
- The existing base is too thin for the load it's carrying
- An overlay would add height that creates transition problems

We'll give you a straight answer on which one your pavement actually needs.
